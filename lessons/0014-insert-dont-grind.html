Insert, Don't Grind:
The First-Layer Corners

You were right — repeating the trigger to drop in a corner is wasteful. Read the corner instead, and one short insertion does the job.

Back in Lesson 2 you learned to park a white corner above its slot and repeat the trigger R U R' U' until it fell in solved — 1, 3, or 5 times. It always works, but it's a grind: up to 20 face turns for a single corner, with a squint after every round to see if you're done.

This is the same upgrade you already made on the last layer: stop blindly repeating, read the case, fire the one thing that fits. The first layer has only three cases, and two of them are three moves long.

The grip does not change. White on the bottom, yellow centre on top, Front toward your chest — exactly the solving grip from every other lesson. You never rotate the cube mid-insertion. Everything below assumes the corner is parked at the top-front-right, directly above its empty home slot.

1. Park first — same as before

Find a white corner in the top layer. Turn the top (U only) until its two side colours sit over their matching centres — it's now directly above its home slot at the front-right. Nothing new yet; this is parking. The new part is what you do instead of grinding.

2. Read the white sticker — three cases

With the corner parked at the top-front-right, the white sticker can only be in one of three places. That's the whole decision.

Why these two are mirror images. R U R' opens the slot to the right, slides the corner over, closes it. F' U' F is the exact same idea opening the slot to the front instead. Whichever face the white sticker is hugging, you open that face. The classic source states it in just these two moves plus a reduction for the third case — Chang & Garron.

The awkward one: white on top

White-on-top is the only hard case, because the white sticker is facing the wrong way entirely. The fix is a setup: R U2 R' spins the corner up and over so white lands on a side, and the rest — U' R U R' — re-parks it and tucks it in. Seven moves, run as one flowing sequence. (Its mirror, F' U2 F U F' U' F, is the same thing on the front — use whichever hand you prefer.) Even this worst case beats the 5-rep, 20-move grind.

3. How much you just saved

The grind averaged about 12 face turns per corner (4, 12, or 20). The read-and-insert method averages roughly 4 (3, 3, or 7) — and there's no stop-and-check between rounds, so it flows. Across all four corners that's the difference between a stuttering ~48 turns and a smooth ~16. That smoothness is the mission: a more elegant solve, not a faster stopwatch.

Sanity-check the F. The front-face case uses F' (front turned counter-clockwise, as if unscrewing a jar lid facing you). Get the direction wrong and you'll feel the bottom layer break. If unsure, do F then F' first and watch which way the front turns — the same ritual from the last-layer lessons.

The rescue case still applies

White corner stuck in the bottom layer (wrong slot, or right slot but twisted)? Same as Lesson 2: hold it at front-right, do one R U R' to pop it into the top, then read it as one of the three cases above.

Practice — before the next session

1. Scramble → cross → all four corners, using only reads and inserts. No grinding the trigger.

2. Say the case out loud as you park each corner: "right," "front," or "top" — then fire. Naming it is the skill.

3. Once it flows, time nothing — just notice the first layer now goes in without a single stop-and-squint.

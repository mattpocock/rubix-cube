What F2L Actually Is

Lessons 15–18 threw you straight into pairs, scoops and triggers. Let's stop and build the picture underneath them — slowly, in real colours. Once this clicks, those four lessons become obvious.

You told me F2L isn't landing — and you're right that something's missing. It isn't the moves (you already own them). It's the mental model they sit on. So forget the trainers for now. This lesson has no new algorithm. Its only job is to make you see what F2L is, so the rest stops feeling like a pile of unrelated tricks.

1. The cube is three layers — and two of them are the job

Hold the cube the way you always solve: white on the bottom, yellow on top — the solving grip you've used since lesson 2. The grip never changes in this lesson. Stacked top to bottom, the cube is three slices:

TOP — yellow layer · your free workbench
▲ scratch space — you don't care what's up here yet
BOTTOM TWO — white layer + middle band · the goal

The goal — bottom two layers

The white face and the band of edges just above it. "First Two Layers" is literally these two slices, solved together. When they're done, only the yellow top is left — and you already own OLL + PLL for that.

The workbench — top layer

Free space. You bring pieces up here, assemble them, and drop them down into the goal. Nothing up top is "wrong" — it's where work happens. This is the idea every F2L move depends on.

Why this is the big win. Your old solve did the bottom two layers in two separate passes — stage 2 placed all four corners, then stage 3 threaded the four middle edges, and every edge insert briefly knocked a corner back out. F2L does the bottom two layers in one pass, with nothing re-disturbed. [Ruwix]

2. The goal breaks into four identical little jobs

Look at the bottom two layers as four corners, each with the edge standing right beside it. That corner-spot plus its neighbouring edge-gap is one slot. Four corners → four slots → four identical jobs. Fill all four and the first two layers are done.

You always work the slot at the front-right, and rotate the whole cube to bring each of the four there in turn — exactly like you already do. Here's that front-right slot, empty, with its real centre colours showing:

The dashed column is the empty front-right slot. The green centre sits to its front, the red centre to its right, yellow on top.

3. Each slot wants exactly two pieces — and you can name them by colour

This is the part the earlier lessons skipped over, and it's the heart of everything. A slot sits between two faces — the front-right slot lives between the green face and the red face. So the only two pieces that belong in it are the two pieces wearing those colours:

The corner for this slot
The one corner with white + green + red. (Every bottom corner has white — it's the bottom face — plus the two slot colours.)
The edge for this slot
The one edge with green + red — no white, no yellow. The middle-band edge that shares the slot's two colours.
The pairing rule, once and for all. A slot's two pieces are the ones that carry its two side colours. The corner = white + those two; the edge = just those two. That corner and that edge are partners — together they're a pair. Move the slot to a blue-and-red gap and the rule is identical: you want the white-blue-red corner and the blue-red edge.

4. The one idea of F2L: join first, then drop together

Here's the whole method in two pictures. The old way put the corner in, then — to add the edge — had to pull the corner back out and rebuild it. Wasted work. F2L snaps the partners together up on the workbench first, then drops both at once into the slot. One motion, nothing disturbed.

The white-green-red corner and the green-red edge, sitting together up top — a joined pair — right above their empty slot. A single insert carries both down. That is F2L.

So what were lessons 15–18? Every one of them is just how to reach that joined-pair picture from a different starting mess — then the same little insert finishes it. Re-read them with this lens and they'll click:
  • Lesson 15 — the pair is already joined and ready → just insert it (R U R' / F' U' F).
  • Lesson 16 — both pieces are up top but apart → "scoop" them into a joined pair, then insert.
  • Lesson 17 — the edge is trapped down in the middle → evict it up to the workbench first (R U R'), then it's a lesson-16 case.
  • Lesson 18 — a slot looks full but is wrong → empty it (R U R'), then rebuild the pair.

5. One win: name a slot's two pieces

No moves to drill today — just the rule from §3, because everything else stands on it. A slot shows its two side colours; pick the corner + edge that belong in it. The right pair wears exactly those two colours (corner adds white). Guided shows the rule; Blind makes you call it. Goal: five in a row.

Practice — this session & after

Now, on the cube — no algorithms: solve your white cross. Then point at each of the four slots in turn and say out loud the two pieces it wants ("this slot is blue–orange → white-blue-orange corner + blue-orange edge"). Then physically find those two pieces anywhere on the cube. That's the whole foundation — locating partners.

Then re-read Lesson 15 with §4's picture in mind. It should read completely differently now.

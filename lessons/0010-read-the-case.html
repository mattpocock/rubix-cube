Rubik's Cube · Lesson 10 · Optimising

Read the Case, Don't Hunt for It

Your first optimisation isn't faster hands — it's a faster eye. The seconds you lose aren't in the turning. They're in the staring.

You can solve the cube. So watch yourself do it: the turning is quick and continuous, then — stop. You hold the cube up, rotate it around, hunt for the piece, work out which case you're in, and only then move again. Those frozen moments are where a solve actually goes slow. Speedcubers have a name for the skill that kills them: look-ahead — but the beginner version is simpler. It's just recognition: naming the situation in one glance instead of puzzling it out.

The optimisation, in one line: the pause is the look, not the turn. You already own every algorithm. What's left to sharpen is the half-second before each one — and that's pure pattern recognition, no new memorisation.

This lesson drills that on the single richest target in the whole solve: the yellow cross (stage 4, F R U R' U' F'). It's the best place to start because it has only three patterns, they're unmistakable once named, and reading them right doesn't just save staring time — it saves whole applications of the algorithm.

1. Three patterns. That's the entire stage.

Look only at the four yellow edges around the centre (ignore corners completely). There are exactly three things you can see — plus the finished cross:

Dot
No edges up. 2 applications.
The L
Two adjacent edges. 1 application.
The Line
Two opposite edges. 1 application.
Cross
All four. Done — 0.

The only judgement call is adjacent vs opposite: two lit edges next to each other form an L; two facing across the centre form a line. Everything else is "no edges" (dot) or "all edges" (cross). That's the whole recognition. [J Perm] [Ruwix]

2. Reading it sets up the cheapest path

Here's why recognition pays double. F R U R' U' F' climbs the ladder dot → L / line → cross — but only if you hold the pattern the right way round first. Hold it wrong and you fall back down to a dot and waste a whole application. So the read tells you both how many applications and how to grip:

You seeHold it like thisCost
LineTurn U until the line is horizontal (left↔right), then run the algorithm.1×
The LTurn U until the two lit edges point up and left (the bend sits in the back-left corner), then run it.1×
DotRun it once from any angle — you'll get an L or a line — then read again and finish.2×
Built-in self-check. If a line or L doesn't go straight to the cross, you held it 180° off — that's the only way to overshoot the costs above. Reset with a U2 (the cross step only cares about how the top is rotated) and run it again. Get the grip right and the stage is deterministic: you know you're done before you start.

3. Drill the read until it's instant

Below, a random top-face flashes up. Name the case as fast as you can — Dot, L, Line, or Cross. Correct answers lock in green and show you the grip-and-cost coaching; a wrong one resets your streak. Don't count edges one by one — let the shape hit you. Goal: eight correct in a row.

What you're training is the move before the move — the instant decision that used to be a pause. Do enough reps and the case will announce itself the moment yellow comes up top, with no conscious counting at all.

4. The same eye works everywhere

Recognition isn't a yellow-cross trick — it's the whole optimisation, and you've already been doing lighter versions of it. Once the cross read is instant, the same "glance, don't hunt" habit collapses the other pauses too:

  • Stage 5 — align the cross. Before any turning, count how many cross edges already match their centre. It's always 0, 2, or 4. Four means skip the stage. Two means turn the matched pair to the back-and-right and you're one edge-swap from done — no trial and error.
  • Stage 6 — position the corners. Audit how many corners are already home: always 0, 1, or 4. One-correct tells you exactly where to grip; zero tells you to just run the cycle once and re-audit. You stop guessing which corner goes where.
  • Stage 7 — orient the corners. Glance at how many corners still need twisting before you start, so the "borrowed pieces" chaos mid-stage never makes you doubt you're on track.

Every one of these is the same skill: read the small number first, then act with certainty. That certainty — not faster fingers — is what makes a solve feel smooth.

Practice — this session & after

Now: trainer to eight in a row, then on a real cube reach a yellow-cross case and say the name and the cost out loud before you turn — "Line, horizontal, one." Do five solves narrating just that one read.

Next few solves: add the stage-5 and stage-6 counts out loud too (the 0/2/4 and 0/1/4 audits). Narrating the read is what turns it from "work it out" into "just see it."

Watch for: your pauses shrinking. When the cube stops freezing between stages, the optimisation has landed — and we can talk about trimming wasted moves next (lesson 11).

Rubik's Cube · Lesson 9

The Corner Cycle

The last peek standing. One algorithm between you and a fully unaided solve — so let's stop memorising eight moves and start memorising four.

You can solve the cube. By your own account every stage is in your hands now except this one — the merry-go-round, U R U' L' U R' U' L. That's no accident: it's the longest algorithm in the method, it shows up only once per solve so it gets the fewest reps, and it's the only one that uses both the right and left faces. Lesson 8 called it months ago — this was always going to be the final holdout.

So this lesson does one thing: drives this single algorithm from your head into your hands. No new theory. Just a structure to hang it on, and a tight loop to drill it.

1. The trick: it's four moves twice

Eight moves is too many to hold as eight separate things. But look at what's actually there — the algorithm is one four-move phrase, played twice, with one tiny edit the second time:

Half A — learn this
U R U' L'
"Up, Right, back-Up, tuck-Left."
Half B — get it free
U R' U' L
Same dance — flip the prime on each side move.

The grey U-beat never changes: U _ U' _, both halves. It's the same heartbeat you already feel in the trigger. The only things that move are the two side grabs — and Half B is just Half A with both side-primes flipped: R becomes R', L' becomes L.

So you memorise four moves and a rule. Recite Half A — U R U' L' — then say "and again, side-primes flipped." That's the whole algorithm. If you ever blank mid-solve, you only have to remember where you are: still in Half A, or replaying it inverted.

And the meaning is the one you already own from Lesson 6: each half lifts the top, swings a corner onto the right, parks one on the left. Half A loads the merry-go-round; Half B's flipped grabs lock the three corners into their new seats and hand back everything the algorithm borrowed. The corner at top-front-right never moves — it's the still post the others rotate around.

3. Take it to the cube

The trainer drills the sequence. Your hands still need the turns. Once you can hit five blind runs above, put the screen down and pick up the cube:

  • Solved cube, slow loop. From solved, run U R U' L' U R' U' L once, watching three corners swing while top-front-right holds still. Keep running it — the cube cycles and comes back to solved after a few rounds, so you can drill on a loop without ever re-scrambling (the same self-scrambler trick from Lesson 8).
  • Burst it. Ten to twenty runs back-to-back, saying the two halves aloud — "U R U' L'… and again, flipped." This is the rep count that pushes an algorithm from head to hands.
  • Drop the voice. Once your hands lead and the words trail, do a few more silent. That's the moment it's truly yours.
  • Prove it in a real solve. Scramble, solve, card face-down. When you reach the corners, run it from memory. If it comes without a peek — that's the mission, fully complete.

Practice — this session & after

Now: trainer to five blind runs in a row, then 15–20 burst reps on a solved cube.

Each day: one face-down-card solve. The corner cycle should be the peek that finally drops to zero.

When it does: you've solved a Rubik's Cube unaided, start to finish — exactly the thing you set out to do. Come tell me and we'll mark the mission done (and talk about whether you want to stop there).

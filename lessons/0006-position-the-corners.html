Rubik's Cube · Lesson 6

Position the Corners: Right Place, Any Twist

Four corners left on the whole cube. This lesson gets each one into its home slot — twisted is fine, that's the final lesson's job.

The grip does not change. Yellow on top, white down, F at your chest. From your aligned cross, the entire unsolved cube is now four corner pieces. Solving them happens in two stages, and it's crucial to keep them separate in your head:

First get every corner into the right slot — even if it sits twisted, yellow facing sideways. Then (next lesson) twist them in place. This lesson is only about the first half: position, not orientation.

1. Reading a corner: "right place" without "right way up"

A corner is in the correct position if its three sticker colours match the three faces it touches — in any arrangement (Ruwix, step 6). Example: the corner between the red and green faces must be the yellow-red-green corner. If those are its three colours, it's home — even if the yellow sticker points sideways at you.

So the check is: pick a corner slot, name the three colours it sits between (yellow + the two side centers), and see whether the piece in it has exactly those three colours. Ignore completely which way they face.

Check yourself

The slot between the blue and red faces holds a corner showing red on top, yellow towards you, blue to the side. Is it positioned correctly?

2. The algorithm — a three-corner merry-go-round

U R U' L' U R' U' L
Cycles three corners · the FRONT-RIGHT corner stays put

What it does: holds the top-front-right corner fixed and cycles the other three top corners around (Ruwix, step 6). That fixed corner is the whole strategy: park a corner that's already correct at front-right, and the algorithm shuffles only the wrong ones.

The shape is friendly: eight moves, alternating top, side, top, side — right hand, then left. First half U R U' L', second half the same dance with the primes swapped on the sides: U R' U' L. Say it as: "U R, U-prime L-prime — U R-prime, U-prime L."

L is back — direction check. You met L in the left insert of Lesson 3: it's the left face, turning clockwise as you look at it from the left side — which means the front-left column moves down towards you‑ish. L' brings it up. Same ritual as ever: L then L' should change nothing. With both R and L in one algorithm, a flipped direction is the likeliest error here.

3. The procedure

  • Audit all four corners. For each, name the slot's three colours and check the piece. Count how many are correctly positioned: 0, 1, or 4. (Exactly 2 or 3 can't happen on a healthy cube — the mirror of the edge rule from Lesson 5.)
  • Four correct: done — go twist them (next lesson).
  • One correct: rotate the whole cube so that corner sits at top-front-right. Run the algorithm. Re-audit: if not all four are home yet, run it once more from the same grip.
  • Zero correct: run the algorithm once from any grip — it will put one corner home. Then handle it as the one-correct case.
Why "run it again" works. The algorithm cycles three corners one step around their merry-go-round. With three corners wrong, they're either one step or two steps away from home — so one run fixes everything, or a second identical run finishes the job. No re-reading needed between runs: same grip, same moves.

4. What done looks like

Every corner is in its home slot, but some (often all) sit twisted — yellow stickers pointing sideways. The top face is a yellow cross plus a few scattered yellow corner stickers; the sides show their T-shapes with possibly-wrong corner stickers in the top row. That's the finish line for this stage. Don't chase the twists — that's the last algorithm of the whole solve, coming next.

The test is colour-sets, not faces. Walk each corner: do its three colours match its three faces? Four yeses = stage complete, regardless of how scrambled it still looks. This is the only stage of the solve where "done" can look messy — trust the audit, not the aesthetics.

Practice — do this before the next session

1. From your current cube: audit the corners out loud, then position them. Verify with a second audit.

2. The self-scramble trick from Lesson 5 works here too: from positioned corners, run the algorithm once to break them, then fix them again. Loop it — only unsolved-stage pieces move, so the rest of the cube is safe.

3. Two fresh scrambles all the way to positioned corners. Notice the audit always finds 0, 1, or 4 correct — never 2 or 3.

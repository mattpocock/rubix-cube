Hooks for the Long Algorithms

You know both PLL algorithms — but they're the longest you've met, and they're still in your head, not your hands. This lesson gives each one a hook, so it comes out in one piece.

The two PLL algorithms are nine and eleven moves — far longer than anything before them. Trying to recall a long algorithm move-by-move is like reciting a phone number you never grouped: the middle falls out. The fix is the same trick your phone uses, and the same one you've already used on the cube without naming it.

Don't memorise moves — memorise chunks. Your working memory holds only a handful of items at once, so long strings overflow it. Group the moves into a few familiar bundles and you're holding three or four things, not eleven. [Chunking — the psychology] You did this on day one: R U R' U' isn't four moves to you, it's one thing — the trigger. The Sune is just the trigger plus a tail. We'll do the same to both PLLs.

A chunk is a short group of moves your fingers run as a single unit. The skill in this lesson isn't new moves — it's seeing each long algorithm as a handful of chunks with a rhythm. Learn that once and every future algorithm gets shorter.

1. The corner algorithm — "Two Walls"

Here is the A-perm, written as your fingers should feel it. The two B2 turns are loud, heavy walls — turning the whole back face halfway round. They split the algorithm into three rooms:

R' F R'knock
B2wall
R F' R'twin knock
B2wall
R2slam

Rhythm: knock-knock-knock — WALL — knock-knock-knock — WALL — SLAM.

The two knocks are twins. Room 1 is R' F R'; room 3 is R F' R'. To get the twin: flip the first two moves, keep the tail R'. So you don't store two rooms — you store one, plus the rule "flip the front pair." Then a single R2 slams the door. Five things to hold becomes really three: knock, wall, slam.

Drill the order of the chunks below — not the individual moves (you have those). Tap the bundles in sequence. Start Guided, then switch to Blind and rebuild it from the hook. Goal: five blind runs in a row.

2. The edge algorithm — "Two See-Saw Bars"

The U-perm has a gift built in: it is nothing but R and U — the two friendliest turns on the cube, the same fingers as the trigger. Read it as two matching bars of R U R U, then a short landing:

R U' R Ubar one
R U R U'bar two
R' U' R2landing

Rhythm: R-U-R-U — R-U-R-U — and down. Same hand the whole way.

The bars see-saw. Both bars are the identical R U R U skeleton — only the U directions flip between them. Bar one dips first (U' then U); bar two rises first (U then U'). Down-up, then up-down — a see-saw. Then the landing R' U' R2 is the only place a prime and the double turn appear. Three chunks: bar, bar, land.

Same drill — tap the three chunks in order, Guided then Blind, five in a row.

3. The shape they share

Notice both hooks land on the same shape — the one you already know from the Sune:

Twin + tail. Each long algorithm is two near-identical chunks plus a short closer. Corners: knock, twin knock, slam. Edges: bar, see-saw bar, landing. When a future algorithm looks frightening, hunt for its twin first — the repeated piece is usually most of it, and what's left is a short tail.

That's the transferable win. You're not just learning two algorithms today; you're learning how to swallow a long algorithm whole — so the next one costs you three chunks, not eleven moves.

How the hook hands off to the cube. On the cube you still recognise the case the normal way — headlights to the back, then the corner alg; solved edge to the back, then the edge alg. The hook is only for producing the moves once you've decided to fire. Say the rhythm under your breath as you turn: "knock, wall, knock, wall, slam." Spoken rhythm survives pressure better than a silent list.

Practice — this session & after

Now: both chunk trainers to five blind runs. Then pick up the cube and run each PLL from a full-yellow top, saying the rhythm aloud as your hands go. If a chunk smears, slow down and name it before you turn.

Between sessions (spacing & interleaving): don't drill one alg ten times then the other — alternate them, and mix them into full OLL → PLL solves so recall happens cold, the way it will on a real scramble.

When both come out of your hands without a peek: tell me. That's the signal the intermediate last layer is truly banked — we'll retire beginner stages 5–7 on your solve card for good.

Rubik's Cube · Lesson 4

The Yellow Cross: One Algorithm, Three Shapes

The last layer begins. Six moves you already half-know, repeated until a cross appears.

Two layers down. Everything that's left to solve lives in the top layer — and the first job is to get the four yellow edge stickers facing up, forming a yellow cross on the top face. Same grip as always: white on the bottom, yellow on top.

One thing changes from here on: you stop hunting for pieces. The last layer isn't solved by finding a piece and steering it home — it's solved by reading the pattern on the top face and applying the matching algorithm. Look, match, execute.

1. The algorithm — you already know the middle of it

F R U R' U' F'
Front clockwise · your old trigger · front counterclockwise

Look at what's inside: R U R' U' — the exact trigger that built your white corners in Lesson 2, and that you've done a hundred times since. The whole algorithm is just that trigger wrapped in an F-sandwich: open with F, do the trigger, close with F'. Say it as: "F — trigger — F-prime." That's the entire memorisation load of this lesson.

What is it actually doing? The cross edges are already in the top layer — they have nowhere else to be. The problem is some of them are flipped: yellow sticker facing sideways instead of up. The F-sandwich flips edges in place. The opening F tips edges into the right hand's reach, the trigger re-seats them the other way up, and the closing F' puts everything back. You're not moving edges around the cube — you're rolling them over where they sit. (More at Ryan Heise's beginner solution.)

2. Read the top face: dot, L, or line

Ignore the corners completely — yellow corner stickers will be scattered around and that's fine, they're the next lesson's job. Look only at the four edge positions on the top face. You'll see one of exactly three shapes (or the cross itself, in which case you're done):

Dot — apply 3×
No yellow edges up. Any way you hold it is fine.
L-shape — apply 2×
Hold it so the L points to the back-left: one arm at the back, one on the left — like a 9 on a clock face.
Line — apply 1×
Hold it so the line runs horizontally, left to right.
Cross — done ✓
All four edges yellow-side-up. Move on.

The three shapes are really one journey: dot → L → line → cross. Each application of the algorithm moves you one step along the chain. That's why the counts are 3, 2, 1 — they're just "how far from the end are you?"

Orientation matters, so re-read between applications. After each run of the algorithm, the shape changes — and the new shape needs to be held its own way before you go again. Dot: run it, find the L, point the L back-left, run it, get the line, turn it horizontal, run it once more. Don't blindly fire three times from one grip. (J Perm and Ruwix both teach exactly this re-read.)

3. Two traps

The F-direction trap, again

Same warning as Lesson 3, because it's still the number-one error: F is clockwise as you look straight at the front face — the top row swings right. The closing F' must be the exact undo. If your first layer "explodes" during this step, a reversed F is almost certainly why. Sanity check: F then F' with nothing in between should change nothing.

One or three yellow edges up

If you ever see exactly one or three yellow edges facing up — not zero, two, or four — stop. That state is impossible on a healthy cube. It means an edge piece was popped out and reinserted flipped at some point (it happens, especially on looser cubes). The fix is physical, not algorithmic: pop the flipped edge out and reseat it the right way round, or take the top layer apart and rebuild. (Both J Perm and Ruwix flag this.) No algorithm will ever get you out of it.

4. What done looks like

Top face: yellow cross. Corners still random — correct.
Sides: two-row walls still intact underneath.
The cross edges don't need to match the sides yet. Yellow up is the whole goal of this step. Lining the cross edges up with their side centers — and sorting the corners — is the rest of the last layer, coming next.

Practice — do this before the next session

1. Solve to the end of the second layer, then make the yellow cross. Note which shape you started from (dot, L, or line).

2. Repeat from a fresh scramble until you've personally seen all three starting shapes at least once. The dot is the rarest — be patient.

3. Each time, before running the algorithm, say the shape and the count out loud: "L, back-left, twice." Reading the pattern is the skill — the moves are ones you already own.

Pairs, Not Pieces

The single biggest inefficiency left in your solve is that you build the first two layers in two separate passes. F2L does them as one — and the insert is a move you already own.

You picked the big one. Right now your solve has two early stages that fight each other: stage 2 drops the four white corners, then stage 3 threads the four middle edges — and every middle-edge insert temporarily yanks a corner back out and puts it back. Eight operations, lots of repaired work. F2L (First Two Layers) replaces both with four clean moves. [Ruwix]

The whole idea in one sentence. A white corner and the middle edge that belongs beside it form a natural pair. Instead of solving them on two separate visits, you join them in the top layer and drop the pair into its slot in one motion. Four pairs → the entire bottom two layers, done together. [speedcube.us]

Your solve now

Stage 2: place 4 corners. Stage 3: place 4 edges, each insert disturbing a corner. Two passes, ~8 operations.

With F2L

4 corner+edge pairs, each joined and slotted in one go. One pass, 4 operations, nothing re-disturbed.

1. Nothing changes about your grip

Good news first: F2L uses the exact grip you already solve in — white cross on the bottom, yellow up, the solving grip you've held since lesson 2. The white cross stays as its own first step, unchanged. You build each pair on your workbench (the top layer) and drop it into the empty slot at the front-right-bottom corner of the cube. That's the only slot you ever insert into — you rotate the whole cube to bring each slot to the front-right.

2. The insert is already in your hands

Here's why this is the right next step and not a mountain. When a pair is already joined in the top layer with its white sticker facing out to a side, sliding it home is the very same corner insert you learned in lesson 14 — it just carries the edge down with it: [Ruwix]

the Right faceR U R'

The insert isn't new. The skill is the pairing. R U R' and F' U' F are exactly the moves that dropped a lone corner in lesson 14. The only difference: park the joined pair so the corner sits above its slot with white on the side, and its edge rides along on top — one insert sends both home. So your entire job in F2L is getting the pair joined and parked; the finish is muscle memory you already have.

3. Ready, or not yet?

Because the insert is free, the real skill this lesson is recognition: is the pair actually ready to go in? A pair is ready when three things are true — the corner is parked above its slot, its white faces a side (not up), and the matching edge is sitting in the top layer right alongside it, continuing the pair's two colours. If white is pointing up, or the corner and edge are scattered apart, it's not ready — that's the joining skill, and it's the whole of the next lesson.

Train that judgement now. A pair-state appears; tap the right call. R U R' or F' U' F if it's ready to insert, or Build first if it isn't. Start guided, then go blind. Goal: five in a row.

In Guided mode the state spells out the answer; in Blind it just describes what you'd see on the cube, and you make the call. The "Build first" cases — white-up and split pairs — are next lesson's job; for now, just naming them is the win.

4. Take it to the cube

  • Make your white cross as normal. Then stop — don't run stage 2 yet.
  • Hunt for a ready pair. Look in the top layer for a white corner already joined to its edge, white facing out. Rotate the whole cube so its empty slot is front-right, park it, and fire R U R' or F' U' F. A whole pair drops in at once — feel how much cleaner that is than corner-then-edge.
  • Insert every ready pair you can find. Some slots will hand you a ready pair; grab those wins.
  • Fall back for the rest — this week only. For pairs that aren't ready (white up, or pieces split), just finish that slot the old way: your stage-2 corner insert, then the stage-3 edge insert. You lose nothing, and next lesson replaces this fallback with proper pairing.
Keep your full solve working. Exactly like the Sune and PLL roll-outs: build the new skill in parallel, don't bet a solve on it. Insert the ready pairs with F2L, finish everything else the way you already can. When spotting and slotting a ready pair feels automatic, tell me — that's when we teach the join, and F2L starts replacing stages 2 and 3 for good.

Practice — this session & after

Now: trainer to five blind calls in a row, then on the cube insert at least three ready pairs across a few scrambles (cross → find a ready pair → R U R' / F' U' F).

Watch one walkthrough: see a ready pair joined and inserted on a real cube — the visual locks in faster than words.

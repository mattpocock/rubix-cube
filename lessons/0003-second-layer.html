Rubik's Cube · Lesson 3

The Second Layer: Two-Thirds Solved

Four edges, two mirror-image algorithms, and the cube goes from one-third to two-thirds done.

Keep the solving grip from Lesson 2: white on the bottom, yellow on top. Your job this lesson is to fill in the middle layer — the four edge pieces that sit between the centers. Once they're in, only the top (yellow) layer remains.

1. Which pieces you're hunting

The middle layer needs exactly four edges: red-green, green-orange, orange-blue, and blue-red. Notice what's not in that list — yellow. Every edge that belongs in the middle layer has no yellow sticker.

So the hunt is simple: look at the edges in the top layer and find one without yellow on either of its two stickers. Edges with yellow live in the last layer — leave them alone, they're next lesson's problem.

Check yourself

You're scanning the top layer and see three edges: yellow-green, red-blue, and yellow-orange. Which can you insert into the middle layer?

2. Park it: make the upside-down T

Found a non-yellow edge in the top layer? Each edge has two stickers: one facing out (to the side) and one facing up. Turn the top layer (U moves only) until the outward-facing sticker matches the center directly below it. Then rotate the whole cube so that face is towards you.

Front face: edge sticker, matching center, and the solved bottom row form an upside-down T

That upside-down T is your "parked" position. Now look at the edge's other sticker — the one facing up. It matches either the center on your right or the center on your left. That tells you which slot the edge is going into, and which algorithm to use.

3. The two inserts

Top sticker matches right center → Right insert

U R U' R'  ·  U' F' U F

The edge drops into the slot between the front and right centers.

Top sticker matches left center → Left insert

U' L' U L  ·  U F U' F'

The exact mirror image, done with the left hand. The edge drops into the slot between the front and left centers.

New letter: F is the front face — the face pointing at you, the one with the upside-down T. It becomes F the moment you park the edge, and stays F for all eight moves: don't re-rotate the cube mid-algorithm. F is a quarter-turn clockwise as you look straight at it — the top row of the front face swings to the right. F' swings it to the left. Direction check before you start: do F then F' — the cube must come back exactly to where it was. A reversed F is the number-one reason this algorithm "destroys" the first layer.

Eight moves sounds like a lot, but look at the shape: each algorithm is two four-move chunks, and every chunk has the same do-undo rhythm as the trigger you already know — turn the top, turn a face, undo the top, undo the face. Say the right insert as two breaths: "U, R, U-prime, R-prime" … "U-prime, F-prime, U, F".

U R U' R' U' F' U F
Right insert — chunk one with the right hand, chunk two on the front face
Two memory hooks. First: the algorithm starts by steering the edge away from the slot it's heading into — right insert begins U (edge swings left), left insert begins U' (edge swings right). Counter-intuitive, so trust it. Second: the chunks alternate hands — right insert is right-hand chunk, then front chunk; the left insert mirrors everything, including every turn direction.
Mid-algorithm, the cube looks broken. It isn't. Around move four or five, a white corner gets yanked out of your finished first layer. That's the algorithm working: it pulls the corner out of the slot, attaches the edge to it, and puts the pair back as one unit. Finish all eight moves and the white layer comes back intact — with the edge installed above it. If you stop halfway in a panic, that's when you lose the first layer.

Check yourself

You've parked the green-red edge: green faces you over the green center, red faces up, and the red center is on your left. Which algorithm?

4. The rescue move: edges stuck in the middle

Sometimes an edge is already in the middle layer but wrong — either in the wrong slot, or in the right slot flipped. And sometimes you'll scan the top layer and find no non-yellow edges at all, which means the ones you need are all stuck in the middle.

The fix is the same idea as the corner rescue in Lesson 2: kick it out, then insert it properly. Hold the cube so the stuck edge's slot is at the front-right, and do the right insert — inserting whatever top-layer edge happens to land there. The stuck edge pops out into the top layer. Now it's a normal case: park it, pick the insert, drop it in.

Check yourself

The blue-orange edge sits between the blue and orange centers — right slot, but flipped (blue sticker on the orange side). What's the plan?

5. What done looks like

Each side: bottom TWO rows solid, matching the center
Top face: still a mess — and that's correct
The test is four solid two-row walls. Walk around the cube: every side face should show its bottom two rows solid and matching the center. Only the top layer — the yellow one — remains scrambled. That's two-thirds of the cube solved, and everything left lives in one layer.

Practice — do this before the next session

1. From where your cube is now: finish the first layer, then insert all four middle edges. Check the two-row walls on all four sides.

2. Full scramble → first layer → second layer, three times in a row without help.

3. Drill the right insert until it flows, before leaning on the left one — you can always bring an edge to a right-insert position by rotating the cube. (Many cubers solve the whole middle layer with just the right insert.) Learn the left insert second, as a speed-up.

Next up, Lesson 4: the last layer begins — making the yellow cross on top. From here on it's pure algorithms, but you've already proven you can run those.

Rubik's Cube · Lesson 1

Anatomy, Notation & the White Cross

Stop thinking in stickers, start thinking in pieces — then build the foundation of every solve you'll ever do.

Grab your cube. Seriously — this lesson is useless without it in your hands. By the end of today you will be able to build a white cross on any scrambled cube, which is the first of the seven stages of the full solve.

1. The cube is pieces, not stickers

The single biggest beginner misconception is that you're moving 54 stickers around. You're not. The cube is made of 26 little plastic pieces, and each piece's stickers are glued on forever. A piece with white and red on it will always have white and red on it. You never fix stickers — you move whole pieces into place.

There are exactly three kinds of piece:

6 centers — 1 sticker each
12 edges — 2 stickers each
8 corners — 3 stickers each
The key insight: centers never move relative to each other. They're bolted to the core. The white center is always opposite the yellow center; red is always opposite orange; blue is always opposite green. So the center defines what colour that face will be when solved. "Solving the white face" really means "moving the 8 white pieces to surround the white center."

Try it now: turn any face of your cube and watch the centers. Twist all you like — the six centers never change position relative to each other.

Check yourself

A friend says: "I peeled off two stickers and swapped them to fix my cube." What kind of pieces could they have ruined the cube for, and why is sticker-thinking wrong?

2. Notation: six letters, one apostrophe

Every solution guide on earth uses the same shorthand for moves. Hold the cube still — the letters name the faces from your point of view:

F — Front
the face looking at you
B — Back
the face away from you
U — Up
the top face
D — Down
the bottom face
R — Right
the right-hand face
L — Left
the left-hand face

Three rules for how the letters become moves:

  • A letter alone — R — means turn that face 90° clockwise, as if you were looking directly at that face.
  • A letter with an apostrophe — R' (say "R prime") — means 90° counter-clockwise.
  • A letter with a 2 — R2 — means a half turn (180°, direction doesn't matter).
The trap everyone falls into: "clockwise" is judged as if that face were in front of you. From where you sit, a clockwise R turns the right face away from you at the top, but a clockwise L turns the left face towards you at the top. They look like opposite motions from your chair — because you'd be looking at those faces from opposite sides. When in doubt, physically rotate the cube so the face you're turning points at you, check the direction, then rotate back.

Check yourself

Hold your cube and physically perform R U R' U', then repeat the same four moves five more times (six total). What happens?

That little sequence, R U R' U', is nicknamed the sexy move by cubers. The fact that six repeats restore the cube is your built-in notation test — use it whenever you're unsure of your turning directions.

3. Your first skill: the white cross

The full beginner solve has seven stages. Today is stage one: get the four white edge pieces around the white center, each one also matching the coloured center on its side. Like this:

White cross (white face)
…and each side edge matches its center
Half a cross is no cross: a white "plus" where the side colours don't match their centers is wrong, even though the white face looks right. Remember — pieces, not stickers. The white-red edge has exactly one home: between the white center and the red center.

The daisy technique

Building the cross directly is fiddly. The beginner-friendly route is to build it upside down first — a pattern called the daisy — then convert it. Hold the cube with the yellow center facing up the whole time.

  1. Make the daisy. Get all four white edge stickers around the yellow center, like white petals on a yellow flower. No algorithms here — just turn faces to bring white edges up to the top layer. The only rule: when moving a new white edge up, give the top face a turn first if needed, so you don't knock out a petal you already placed.
  2. Match a petal to its center. Look at the side colour of one petal (the non-white sticker). Turn the top face (U moves only) until that side colour sits directly above the center of the same colour — e.g. the white-red petal above the red center.
  3. Drop it down. Turn that face 180° (e.g. F2 if it's the front face). The petal flips down to the bottom, landing white-side-down next to the white center — exactly where it belongs.
  4. Repeat for the other three petals. Match, drop. Match, drop. Match, drop. Flip the cube over: white cross, side colours matching. Done.
The daisy (yellow face up)

Check yourself

You've made the daisy. One petal is the white-blue edge. What do you do with it?

Practice — do this before the next session

1. Scramble your cube thoroughly (25+ random moves). Build the daisy, then convert it to the white cross. Time doesn't matter; correctness does — check all four side colours match their centers.

2. Repeat until you can do it three times in a row without getting stuck.

3. Stretch goal: as you scramble, call out the notation of each move you make (R, F', U2…) to drill the letters into your hands.

When the cross feels routine, you're ready for Lesson 2: the white corners, which completes the entire first layer — and introduces your first real algorithm.

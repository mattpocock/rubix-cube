Rubik's Cube · Lesson 8

Put Down the Card

You can solve the cube. Now make the solve card unnecessary — by noticing how little there actually is to memorise.

First, perspective: it feels like there's a lot to remember, but the entire method is six algorithms, and they're not six strangers. Half of them are built from one move-pattern you already own cold. The work isn't memorising six things — it's memorising about three and a half.

1. The family tree — the trigger is everywhere

Lay the six out and look for R U R' U'-shaped bones (the community calls these embedded patterns triggers — SpeedCubeShop):

StageAlgorithmWhat to actually remember
2 — White cornersR U R' U'The trigger itself. Already automatic.
3 — Right insertU R U' R' · U' F' U FTwo do–undo breaths: steer away with the right hand, escort home with the front.
3 — Left insertU' L' U L · U F U' F'Nothing new — it's the right insert in a mirror. Every move flips hand and prime.
4 — Yellow crossF R U R' U' F'One word: F-sandwich. The trigger wrapped in F … F'.
5 — Edge swapR U R' U R U2 R' UThe genuinely new one. Rhythm: up-over-down, U, up-over-OVER-down, U. Only R and U.
6 — Corner cycleU R U' L' · U R' U' LThe other new one. Two halves, same dance, side-primes swapped: U R, U' L' — U R', U' L.
7 — Final twistR' D' R DFour moves. Down-pair, up-pair. Barely counts.

So the real memorisation load: the edge swap, the corner cycle, and keeping the two inserts from blurring together. Everything else is the trigger wearing a costume.

2. How memory for this actually works

Three findings that come up again and again from cubers (Cubelelo, SpeedSolving forum):

  • Chunks, not letters. Don't recall eight letters; recall two four-move phrases. You've been doing this since Lesson 3 — the gold-highlighted halves on the solve card are the chunk boundaries. Say the chunks aloud while turning; the voice becomes the retrieval cue.
  • Watch the pieces once. Run each algorithm slowly, once, watching what physically moves — the edge swap shuffling front and left, the merry-go-round circling three corners. A sequence with meaning is recalled by reconstructing the meaning, and you already have the meaning from the lessons. This is your existing superpower: your records show concepts stick instantly for you.
  • Burst reps. 10–20 executions in a row, without stopping, is the standard prescription for pushing an algorithm from head to hands. You have a free tool for this: the self-scrambler loop — the edge swap, corner cycle, and final twist each undo themselves on repetition, so you can drill them on a solved cube endlessly.
Recall beats re-reading. The thing that builds memory is the moment of struggling to retrieve — not the moment of looking at the answer. Reading the solve card ten times does almost nothing; trying to produce the algorithm and then checking does almost everything. Every drill below is built on that: attempt first, card second.

4. Drill 2 — the fading card

This is the bridge from "drilled" to "solving unaided", and it's how you'll spend the next few days:

  • Solve with the card face-down. Scramble, solve, and only flip the card over when you're genuinely stuck — after you've tried to retrieve the algorithm first.
  • Count your peeks. One tally per flip, noting which stage. That number is your score for the solve.
  • Between solves, burst-drill the peeked stage. Whatever you had to look up, run it 10–20 times via the self-scrambler loop before the next scramble.
  • One or two solves a day beats ten in one sitting. Sleep consolidates this kind of memory; spacing the practice is doing invisible work. Three or four days of this typically gets the count to zero.
  • Zero peeks = a fully unaided solve. That's the finish line.
The likely last holdout. Expect the corner cycle (U R U' L' U R' U' L) to be the final peek to die — it's the longest, the least frequent per solve, and the only one using both hands' faces. If it lingers, lean on its symmetry: the second half is the first half with the side-moves primed. You only memorise four moves; the other four are a rule.

Practice — this week

Today: the flashcard drill above, cube in hand, until you clear all seven cards once without flipping early. Then one face-down-card solve to set your baseline peek count.

Each day after: one or two solves, card face-down, tally peeks, burst-drill whatever you peeked at.

When you hit zero peeks: that's the unaided solve — the mission, fully complete. Come tell me, and we'll decide if there's anywhere you want to go from there.

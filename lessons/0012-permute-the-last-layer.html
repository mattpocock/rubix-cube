Permute the Last Layer

Last lesson the top went fully yellow but the pieces sat in the wrong places. This is the lesson that slides them home — and turns your new last layer into a finished cube.

In lesson 11 you learned to orient the last layer — one repeated Sune paints the whole top yellow. But orienting only points every piece up; it doesn't put them where they belong. The corners and edges are yellow-on-top yet still scrambled around the ring. The second half of the intermediate last layer fixes exactly that: permutation — PLL. [Speedsolving Wiki]

Be told the honest price up front. Orientation cost you one algorithm. Permutation costs two: one to cycle the corners, one to cycle the edges. There's no shortcut that reuses something you already own — your old beginner corner move twists pieces, so it can't be used here. But the two new algorithms are twins: they obey the same rule, so you're really learning one idea applied twice.

1. The shape of PLL: corners, then edges

2-look PLL does the layer in two looks, always in this order: place the corners first, then cycle the edges. Each look is one algorithm that cycles three pieces and leaves the fourth alone — a "3-cycle." That fixed fourth piece is your anchor, and the whole method hangs off one rule:

The twin rule — anchor to the back. Find the piece (or pair) that's already finished, hold the cube so it sits at the back, then fire the algorithm. The three unfinished pieces rotate into place; the finished one never moves. If it's not solved after one go, re-look and fire again — never more than twice.

That "never more than twice" isn't a hope — it's been checked exhaustively against every possible last-layer arrangement. Two algorithms, each fired once or twice, finish any cube. Here's the pair, side by side; then we'll drill them one at a time.

2. Look 1 — permute the corners

First, get all four corners into their correct corners of the cube (ignore the edges for now). The recognition cue is headlights. By now your top is fully yellow (you've done the Sune), so every side sticker on a top corner is one of the other five colours. Hold the cube at eye level and look at a side face head-on: read only the two outer stickers of its top row — the corners. If those two corners are the same colour as each other, that's a pair of headlights. The middle sticker between them (the "grille") doesn't matter, and the colour doesn't have to match the centre — only each other.

Here's the fact that clears up the confusion — and it's a built-in check, verified against every possible corner arrangement: when you walk around the four side faces, the number that show headlights is always 0, 1, or 4 — never 2 and never 3. So if it looks like three, look again, because that case doesn't exist. You're almost certainly seeing the four-face case (the back one is just hidden — you can only see three sides at once without turning the cube).

New algorithm 1 — the corner permutation (an "A-perm")
R' F R' B2 R F' R' B2 R2
Cycles three corners and leaves the front-left one untouched. Yellow stays on top — it never re-twists what the Sune already fixed.

The action is just the table above: headlights to the back (or fire once to summon them in the diagonal case), then run it. Notice the relief: no D, no whole-cube flips mid-algorithm — just R, F and B faces. [J Perm] Drill it the usual way: green locks each move in, a wrong tap resets the run. Start Guided, then go Blind. Goal: five blind runs in a row.

3. Look 2 — permute the edges

With the corners home, only the edges remain. Now the anchor is a single solved edge: the top edge whose front colour already matches the centre below it. Hold that edge at the back and the algorithm cycles the other three into place.

New algorithm 2 — the edge permutation (the "U-perm")
R U' R U R U R U' R' U' R2
Cycles three edges, leaves the back edge alone, and never touches a corner. R and U faces only — the friendliest fingers on the cube.
You seeDo this
One solved edgeHold it at the back and run the U-perm. If it didn't solve, the cycle ran the other way — just run it once more.
No solved edge at allRun the U-perm once from any angle — that creates a solved edge. Re-aim it to the back and run it again.

Run the U-perm cleanly five times blind, same as before. (The decoy buttons aren't part of this algorithm — part of the drill is not reaching for them.)

4. Put the whole new last layer together

You now own every piece of the intermediate last layer. End to end, after the second layer is done, the last layer is just four looks:

  • Yellow cross — F R U R' U' F' (lesson 4). Orient the edges.
  • Yellow corners — the Sune, count-hold-repeat (lesson 11). Now the whole top is yellow.
  • Place the corners — headlights to the back, A-perm. (new)
  • Cycle the edges — solved edge to the back, U-perm, then a final U to line everything up. (new) Solved.
This is the swap-over. These four looks replace beginner stages 5, 6 and 7 entirely — no more aligning the cross by cases, no more R' D' R D grind, no more orientation-twisting corner cycle. Keep the old method in your back pocket for one more week if you like, but the moment OLL → PLL feels reliable, retire the old last layer for good. Practice — this session & after

Now: each trainer to five blind runs. Then on the cube, solve up to a full yellow top (cross + Sune), and from there practise just the two PLL looks: headlights-to-back A-perm, then solved-edge-to-back U-perm.

Between sessions: do complete OLL → PLL last layers from ten different scrambles. Say the recognition aloud ("headlights at back, A-perm" … "solved edge at back, U-perm"). Let yourself fire an algorithm twice — that's normal, not a mistake.

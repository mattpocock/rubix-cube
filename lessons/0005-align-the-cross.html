Rubik's Cube · Lesson 5

Align the Cross: Making Yellow Edges Honest

The cross is up — now its edges have to tell the truth about their side colours. One new algorithm, made of letters you already own.

The grip does not change. Yellow on top, white on the bottom, F is the face at your chest. That holds for this lesson and every lesson left.

Right now your yellow cross is up, but look at the four side stickers of those cross edges — the colours facing outward. They're almost certainly not matching the centers below them. This stage fixes that: each cross edge ends up sitting above its own center. Red over red, green over green, all the way round.

1. First, spin the top and count

Before any algorithm: turn the top layer (U moves only) one click at a time, and at each click count how many cross edges match the center below them. One of the four positions will give you the best count — and it's always one of these:

All four match — done ✓
Sometimes you get lucky. Skip to the next lesson's territory.
Two adjacent match
The matched pair sit next to each other — e.g. back and right.
Two opposite match
The matched pair face each other — e.g. front and back.
You can always get at least two to match — never exactly one or three. That's not luck, it's how edge arrangements work on the cube: the four edges' positions relative to each other only allow these outcomes. So spin until you find two (or four) matches. If you genuinely can't get two, an edge has been physically popped and reseated wrong at some point — same diagnosis as the impossible cases in Lesson 4.

(These diagrams show the side-facing stickers of the cross edges as if peeled up onto the top — on the real cube you check them by looking at each side face: does the top-middle sticker match the center?)

2. The algorithm — all letters you own

R U R' U R U2 R' U
Swaps the FRONT and LEFT cross edges · only R and U — no F to get wrong

Read it out: "R U R' — U — R U-two R' — U." Nothing but R and U turns, so there's no F-direction trap this time. U2 just means turn the top half a turn — two clicks. The shape to feel: it's almost your trigger, but instead of undoing the top each time, you keep feeding it — up, over, up, over-over, out.

What it does: swaps the front and left cross edges, leaving the back and right edges where they are (Ruwix, step 5). It also shuffles the yellow corners around — which costs you nothing, because the corners are still unsolved anyway. This is a theme for the whole last layer: each algorithm protects what's already done and churns only what isn't.

3. The procedure

  • Spin the top (U clicks) to the position with the most matches. Four? Done. Two? Note whether they're adjacent or opposite.
  • Two adjacent: rotate the whole cube so the matched pair sit at the back and right. The two liars are now at front and left — exactly where the algorithm swaps. Run it once.
  • Two opposite: run the algorithm once from any position. It breaks the standoff — you'll now have an adjacent case. Go back to step 1.
  • Finish: after the swap, spin the top to line everything up and check all four sides. Cross edges done.
This is the second cube-rotation decision of the solve. Like parking edges in Lesson 3, you rotate the whole cube to set up (matched pair to back-and-right), then keep it still for the whole algorithm. Worst case from a fresh cross is two runs: one to break an opposite pair, one to swap the adjacent liars.

4. What done looks like

Top: yellow cross, as before.
Each side: a T shape — two solid rows plus the matching cross edge on top. Only the corners lie.
The test is four T-shapes. Walk around the cube: every side should show its two solved rows plus the top-middle sticker matching. The only unsolved pieces left on the entire cube are the four yellow corners. Two stages remain: put those corners in the right places, then twist them yellow-side-up.

Practice — do this before the next session

1. From your current cube: make the cross, then align it. Verify the four T-shapes.

2. Fresh scramble → everything you know → aligned cross, three times. Before touching the cube each time, say the case out loud: "adjacent — pair to back-right" or "opposite — break it first."

3. Spot-check your spin-and-count: every solve, notice that exactly two or four edges match at the best position — never one or three. Seeing the rule hold builds trust in the read.

Fix a Wrong Slot

A slot can look finished and still be wrong — an edge in backwards, a corner twisted, the wrong piece sitting there entirely. Here's how to spot it in a glance, and the one move that fixes all three.

You asked the sharp question: what do you do when the edge is in the right slot but twisted the wrong way? It's worth its own lesson, because it belongs to a whole family — the filled-but-wrong slot. The slot looks occupied, so it's easy to skate past it, but the colours don't line up, and that pair is not actually solved. The skill here is two halves: reading the tell, and knowing that the fix is something you already do.

1. A solved slot has clean colour runs

Hold the slot at the front-right and look at its two visible faces. When a pair is genuinely solved, the colours run cleanly down the column — the front face of the slot matches the green centre above it, the right face matches the red centre. Top-to-bottom, one colour each:

green — the front face; it should run unbroken down the front of the slot
red — the right face; unbroken down the right of the slot
white — belongs on the bottom; if you can see it on a side, something's twisted
The one check. Solved = each side of the slot is a single clean colour, and no white on the sides. Any break in that pattern means the slot is filled but wrong — and needs fixing, not skipping.

2. The three tells

Three things can be wrong. You don't need to name which — but learning to see them stops you trusting a slot that only looks done:

Solved

Green runs down the front, red down the right. Leave it.

Edge flipped

The edge's two colours are swapped — red interrupts the green run. Right slot, wrong way round.

Corner twisted

White on a side instead of the bottom. The corner's in its slot but rolled the wrong way.

(The fourth case — a completely wrong piece jammed in there — reads the same way: colours that don't belong to this slot at all. Same fix.)

3. One fix for all of them

Here's the relief: you don't repair a wrong slot in place, and you don't need a new move to deal with each tell. You empty the slot and rebuild — and emptying it is the evict you already know:

Clear any wrong slot, then rebuild
R U R'
Hold the wrong slot at front-right, fire once: whatever's jammed in there — flipped edge, twisted corner, both, or the wrong piece — lifts up to the top layer. Now pair it properly (Lesson 16) and insert. The rebuild puts everything back the right way round.
Why there's no "flip it in place" trick. An edge sitting the wrong way round can't be turned over without leaving its slot — the cube simply has no move that twists one piece and touches nothing else. So the honest fix is always the same: take it out, rebuild. Expect this: if a correct piece shared the slot, R U R' lifts it out too. That's not a step backwards — you can't free the wrong piece without briefly opening the slot, and you'll re-insert the whole pair cleanly in seconds.

Checked on the cube simulator: a single R U R' clears the front-right slot — flipped edge, twisted corner, or random junk — and leaves your white cross fully intact every time. [verification]

4. Drill the tell

A slot appears at the front-right. One glance: are the colours clean (leave it) or broken / white-on-side (clear with R U R')? Guided explains; Blind makes you call it. Goal: five in a row.

One that's filled-but-wrong, you now fix on sight. When that check is automatic and all four slots build reliably, tell me — that's the green light to retire stages 2 and 3 on your solve card and fold F2L in for good. The last case still waiting is the same-colour-up "hide and seek" pair.

Practice — this session & after

Now: trainer to five blind calls. Then on the cube, deliberately build the case: solve a slot, pull just the edge and reseat it flipped, then fix it — R U R', re-pair, insert. Do the same with a twisted corner.

Between sessions: at the end of the first two layers, scan all four slots for clean colour runs before moving to the last layer. Catching a wrong slot now saves a broken solve later.
